The persistence question just answered itself with what you described.
The computer IS the persistence mechanism. The player's exo-suit is networked to the surface computer. Every time the player falls or ascends, the computer restores them from the most recent backup it was powerful enough to store. Early on the machine can barely save anything — basic motor calibration, the physical infrastructure already built, the location of portals. As it grows through generations it can store more: skill memory, stat calibrations, equipment configurations, eventually full neural continuity. The player isn't just building a computer. They're building their own immortality engine. Each generation is a more complete version of themselves coming back.
This reframes the whole ascension arc. The reset isn't a game mechanic with a thin narrative excuse — it's the point of the game. You're iterating toward a machine powerful enough to restore you perfectly. The Edge of Tomorrow loop is the story.
The portal system fits into this beautifully too. The computer doesn't just store the player — it calculates and maintains stable connections to other points in reality. A low-generation machine can barely hold a portal to a nearby jungle. A high-generation machine can punch through to a different planet, a different era, a different dimension entirely. Zone unlocks aren't just PP thresholds — they're a direct measure of what the computer is now capable of reaching. The player isn't traveling, they're extending the machine's reach.
And the boss as simulation makes even more sense now. Before the computer can establish a stable portal to a new territory, it needs to model and simulate what's on the other side — including its defenders. The boss fight is the computer running that simulation with the player as the test agent. Pass the benchmark, the portal locks in, the territory opens.
